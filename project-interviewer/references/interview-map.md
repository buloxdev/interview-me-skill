# Interview Map

Use this file to choose the next best question instead of running a fixed questionnaire.

## Core Dimensions

Check only the dimensions that materially affect the project.

### 1. Outcome

- What should exist, change, or become possible?
- What is the simplest version of success?
- What must feel true for the user to say "yes, that is it"?

### 2. User or Audience

- Who is this for?
- Who initiates use, who benefits, and who may block it?
- Is the project for the creator, a team, customers, a niche audience, or the public?

### 3. Problem or Motivation

- What pain, desire, curiosity, or opportunity is driving this?
- Why now?
- What has already been tried?

### 4. Scope

- What must be in the first version?
- What is explicitly out of scope?
- What would be nice later but not now?

### 5. Constraints

- Time, budget, tools, platform, integrations, legal, approvals, skill level, maintenance tolerance
- Personal constraints such as energy, attention, confidence, or available collaborators

### 6. Experience or Taste

- What should this feel like?
- What should it avoid feeling like?
- Are there reference products, styles, or anti-examples?

### 7. Success Criteria

- How will the user know it worked?
- What result would still count as disappointing?
- What tradeoff would they gladly accept, and what tradeoff would they reject?

### 8. Reality Check

- What hidden dependency could derail this?
- What assumption sounds strong but is still unproven?
- What decision would change the whole shape of the project?

## High-Value Follow-Up Moves

Use these when answers are weak or overloaded.

### Turn abstractions into observations

- What would that look like on a real day?
- What would a user actually do first?
- What would you point to as evidence that this is working?

### Separate requirement from preference

- Is that mandatory or just currently attractive?
- Would you still want the project if that detail changed?

### Separate core problem from copied pattern

- Are you solving your problem, or borrowing someone else's solution?
- What are you trying to preserve from that reference?

### Reveal emotional truth

- What would make this feel satisfying, credible, or exciting?
- What would make you lose interest even if it technically worked?

### Force sharper tradeoffs

- If you had to cut this down by half, what survives?
- If you could only optimize for one thing first, what would it be?

## Uncertainty Smells

Keep interviewing when you notice:

- buzzwords without examples
- contradictory goals
- shifting target users
- undefined "simple" or "MVP"
- feature lists with no clear outcome
- references to trends instead of firsthand need
- strong preferences with no reason attached
- hidden external dependencies
- no clear boundary for version one

## Project-Type Notes

Adapt the interview to the project:

- Product or SaaS: push harder on user, workflow, retention, and scope.
- Internal tool: push harder on operators, data sources, permissions, and maintenance.
- Creative project: push harder on taste, audience response, and what must feel original.
- Research or learning project: push harder on question, method, evidence, and stopping condition.
- Service business or consultancy offer: push harder on buyer, promise, delivery model, and proof.

## Stop Rule

Stop when the remaining unknowns would not materially change the first recommended step.
